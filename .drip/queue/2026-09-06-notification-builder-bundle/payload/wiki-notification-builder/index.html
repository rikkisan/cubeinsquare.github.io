How to use the Minecraft Notification Builder

Minecraft gives you several ways to speak to players, but they do not all feel the same. A title can stop a room cold. An actionbar can sit under movement without breaking the scene. Tellraw is better when information should stay readable and calm. This builder exists so you can choose the tone first and only then worry about syntax.

Open notification builder

1. Pick the delivery layer, not just the text

Use a title when the moment needs weight: a duel beginning, a ritual finishing, an evacuation warning, or a quest threshold being crossed. Titles interrupt vision, so they work best for beats that deserve that force.

Use an actionbar when you want information to stay close to the player without stealing the whole screen. Cooldown cues, short warnings, objective nudges, and traversal hints often fit here better than in a title.

Use tellraw when the message needs to read like a system line, a narrator note, or a calmer quest update. It is also easier to chain with clickable chat logic later if you decide to extend the flow.

2. Build the command around the scene

Start with the target selector and message mode, then decide whether the line needs a second beat. A strong title with a subtitle can create a clean two-step rhythm: first impact, then context.

Timing matters more than people expect. Very short fade values make notifications feel abrupt and gamey. Slightly longer fade and stay values help the same message feel intentional inside a roleplay scene.

3. Keep the text readable in motion

The best in-game notices are not usually long. They are shaped for the moment: one strong line, one useful clarification, and a color choice that matches urgency without turning everything into red panic text.

If the message needs paragraphs, it probably wants tellraw or a book page instead of a title. The builder is strongest when it helps you stay concise.

4. Test once in context

A warning that reads well in an empty world can become unreadable during combat or when particles are flying everywhere. Always test at least one live version in the kind of scene where the message will actually appear.

If it feels too loud, move it down from title to actionbar or tellraw. If it feels too quiet, give it a stronger title line and shorten the copy.

FAQ

Should everything be a title?

No. Titles are best for heavy moments. Actionbar and tellraw usually feel better for routine information.

When should I use subtitle?

When the first line carries impact and the second line adds context, direction, or timing.

What if I only need one short warning?

Use actionbar or a single title line. Do not force a subtitle just because the field exists.

Can I use this for onboarding?

Yes. Tellraw and timed title sequences are both good for first-join guidance and event intros.
